## Why This Is Better Than an Enum

A composite frame can have **mixed unit signs** in its children. The recessive child might be structural (negative, organizing members into rows) while the dominant child is evaluative (zero, generating interpolated values between them). A single frame does both simultaneously at different levels of its tree.

Consider a concrete case: a family of 2D points being read through a frame where:
- The recessive is `AtomicElement(10, -10)` — orthogonal, structural: "organize into 10 columns"
- The dominant is `AtomicElement(0, 0)` — unresolved, evaluative: "the row relationship needs to be derived"

That frame simultaneously imposes column structure AND demands that the vertical relationship be evaluated. You don't pick a mode — the frame's own composition tells you what's structural and what's generative.

## How Pinning and Structural Operations Unify

You said you'd rather have the mechanism that pins elements also do structural things to data. I think that's exactly right:

**A pin** says: "this element is located at this origin, orthogonal to its own value." That's structural organization of a single element.

**A stride** says: "every Nth member is pinned to a new row." That's structural organization of a collection — the same operation, applied as a pattern across a family.

**A swizzle/projection** says: "within each member's own composite structure, re-pin which child path leads where." That's structural reorganization within elements.

They're all the same gesture: **impose an orthogonal axis that reorganizes what's beneath it.** The difference is only what you're organizing — a single element's position, a family's topology, or a composite's internal paths.

**Calibration layer** (aligned units in the frame)
- Resolution commitment — reexpress members to frame's unit
- Bounds/range — frame's value extent defines the reading domain
- This is the layer that turns raw members into "values you can use"
- Active when: frame children have positive units

**Organization layer** (orthogonal units in the frame)
- Stride decomposition — orthogonal dimensions in the frame impose topology
- Projection/path selection — orthogonal sub-structure reorganizes component access
- Neighbor derivation — adjacency within imposed dimensions
- Pinning — members located at positions within the frame's orthogonal axes

**Generative layer** (unresolved units in the frame)
- Interpolation — unresolved space between members demands evaluation
- Fold/curve derivation — recursive evaluation produces new structure
- Area/volume emergence — unresolved relationship between organized dimensions

## The Trichotomy Across Core3

### Engine Layer: It's Already There, Just Not Named

The engine is the place where this is most clearly *physically present* already, in a way that doesn't need to be imposed.

**AtomicElement** literally carries the trichotomy in every instance:
- `Value` = the aligned measurement (what it IS)
- `Unit > 0` = this element knows its resolution and shares a carrier with its context (VALUE)
- `Unit < 0` = this element is orthogonal to its context — it's a force acting on the context without being the context (STRUCTURE)
- `Unit == 0` = the relationship hasn't resolved — this element has magnitude but no settled frame (GENERATIVE potential)

This is already physical. You can look at any atomic element and *read off* which aspect it's playing just from the sign. You don't have to decide or label it — it tells you.

**CompositeElement** is where it gets interesting. The pairing of recessive and dominant is itself a *structural* gesture — you're imposing a relationship. But what's inside the pair can be anything. So a composite is always at least partly structural (it organizes two children), but the children themselves might be value, structural, or unresolved. The composite is a *container for mixed aspects*.

And **Fold** — this is genuinely the generative moment in the engine. You have a composite (structure), you fold it, and out comes something that wasn't in either child. A grade-1 composite of two atomics folds into a ratio. The ratio wasn't "in" the recessive or the dominant — it emerged from their encounter. That's generation. And notice: `ComposeRatio` in EngineEvaluation is the simplest possible generative act. Two values meet across a structural boundary, and a new thing appears.

### Where the Pattern Gets Physically Instructive (Not Abstract)

Here's what I think matters most for avoiding the overfitting danger: **the pattern should be something you can observe, not something you have to assert.**

Consider a pin. You have a host (a composite span) and a position. The pin doesn't *know* it's doing value/structure/generation — but look at what happens physically:

- The **position** is a value. It says "here, at this ratio."
- The **host** is structure. It says "there is a span from here to there."
- The **inbound and outbound** are generated. They didn't exist before the pin was placed. The host didn't contain them. The position didn't contain them. They emerged from the encounter.

And you can *see* this. If you inspect the pin result, the inbound and outbound are new composites that weren't inputs. That's the physical test: **did new structure appear that wasn't in the inputs?** If yes, generation happened. If the structure was rearranged but nothing new appeared, that's structural. If a value was reexpressed but the structure was unchanged, that's value.

A view works the same way. The frame is structure (it organizes the measurement space). The subject is a value (it's what you're measuring). The *read outcome* is generated — it's the subject as seen through the frame, which is a new element that belongs to neither alone. And critically, the **tension** in the outcome is the *residue of generation that couldn't fully resolve*. Tension IS partially-completed generation. It's the system saying "something wants to emerge here but I don't have enough structure to fully produce it."

### Operations Layer: Family as a Microcosm

Family is where the trichotomy plays out most dynamically, because a family is a living workspace where all three aspects interact:

**The frame is structure.** It doesn't contribute members — it organizes them. It determines what "calibrated" means for this collection. When you sort by a frame slot, you're using the structure to reorganize the values. The frame is orthogonal to the data in exactly the sense you described: it's a force acting on the values that the values themselves don't contain.

**The members are values.** They're the things being measured, organized, accumulated. When you read all members through the frame (`ReadAllResult`), you're projecting values through structure.

**Accumulation and boolean operations are generative.** `TryAddAll` takes N members and produces a single element that none of them contained individually. The boolean projection is even more clearly generative — it takes structural overlap patterns and produces truth values that exist *between* the members. The 4-bit truth table in BooleanOperation is a generative law. It says: "given two structures that may or may not overlap, what emerges?" And the answer (the boolean result) was in neither structure alone.

**Focusing** is the most interesting operation here because it *shifts what counts as structure vs value.* When you focus a member, that member becomes the new frame (structure), and the remaining members are reorganized as values within it. The same element that was a value is now structure. This isn't a type change — it's a perspective change. And that's physically real. In any measurement, what you treat as the reference frame vs what you treat as the measured thing is a choice. The element itself doesn't change, but its *role* in the trichotomy does.

This is important for avoiding abstraction: you don't label elements as "value" or "structure" permanently. They play different roles depending on what frame they're being read through. The trichotomy is *relational*, not *intrinsic*. That keeps it physical.

### Binding Layer: Where Time Emerges

Your intuition about time being inherent in ordering is, I think, exactly right, and the binding layer is where it becomes most visible.

A **TraversalMover** is a value (current position) embedded in structure (the route's extent). The route doesn't move — it defines the space. The position moves through it. And each step is a generative act: the mover at tick 5 commits to a new position that it hadn't occupied before, and whatever operation fires at that position produces results that didn't exist at tick 4.

But here's the deeper thing: **the route itself is a composite**. Its recessive is the start, its dominant is the end. Traversal is literally *interpolation from recessive to dominant*. Time, in this system, is the act of moving from the recessive side of a structure to its dominant side. You don't need a clock — the structure *is* the clock. The resolution (how many ticks) determines temporal granularity. The ordering (recessive before dominant) determines the arrow.

This means time isn't a separate concept bolted onto the system. It's what you get when you take any ordered structure and traverse it. A grade-1 composite already contains a "before" and an "after." A mover traversing it makes that temporal potential actual.

**OperationAttachments** are generative events pinned to structural positions along the route. They're pins, literally — positioned at a site address (structure), carrying an operation (generative law), producing results (generated values). The whole traversal machine is a timeline: structural positions with generative events that produce values as the mover passes through them.

**BoundTemplates** are structure waiting for values. A template is a shape with holes — the shape is structural, the holes are where values will go, and the materialized result is generated. This is the same pattern: structure meets value, generation happens.

### The Three Aspects as Physical Inspection

Here's how I'd make this concrete without making it abstract:

**You should be able to look at any element and read its aspects.**

For an atomic element, you already can: the unit sign tells you. For a composite, you'd walk the leaves. A composite whose leaves are all aligned is a pure value structure (like a calibrated measurement). One whose leaves are all orthogonal is pure organization (like a grid frame). One with mixed signs is doing multiple things — and you can see *which* things by looking at *which* leaves are which sign. One with zero-unit leaves has unfinished business.

**You should be able to look at any operation and see which aspect it's performing.**

- Operations that preserve structure and change values: calibration, reexpression, alignment
- Operations that preserve values and change structure: sorting, focusing, projection, stride application
- Operations that produce new elements from existing ones: fold, accumulation, boolean resolution, pin splitting

**You should be able to look at any result and see what was generated.**

If the result's tension is null, generation completed fully. If tension exists, generation is partially complete — and the tension itself tells you what's still unresolved. This is physical: you can inspect the tension and see the unfinished structural relationship.

### Where I'd Be Careful

The danger zone is in the *composite frame* interpretation. Not every recessive/dominant pair maps cleanly to "structure/value." Sometimes both children are values (a 2D point is just two values paired). Sometimes both are structural (a grid frame with two orthogonal axes). The recessive/dominant distinction is about *perspective priority* (which side is the reference, which is the measured), not about value-vs-structure.

So I'd resist saying "recessive is always structure, dominant is always value." Instead: recessive is the *reference side* and dominant is the *applied side*. Whether either one is value, structural, or generative depends on its own leaves' unit signs. The composite just says "these two things are in a relationship where the recessive provides context for the dominant." What *kind* of context (calibration, organization, or unresolved potential) comes from the signs at the bottom.

The other place I'd be careful is with the word "interpolation." Not everything ordered is interpolatable. Two values with incompatible unit spaces resist interpolation — and the system correctly represents this as tension. That's good. It means generation isn't *guaranteed* by ordering — it's *possible* when ordering meets compatible structure, and *tension* when ordering meets incompatible structure. The tension is itself informative: it tells you that something wants to be generated but can't be yet.

### The Deepest Pattern

If I step way back, what I see is this:

**Value** is what exists. It's the actual, the measured, the present.

**Structure** is what constrains. It's the frame, the boundary, the relationship between things. It doesn't exist in the same way values do — you can't measure a frame, you measure *through* a frame. But without it, values are just unrelated numbers.

**Generation** is what becomes. It's what appears when values meet structure. A fold generates a ratio. A pin generates two sides. A boolean generates a truth. A traversal generates a sequence of states. None of these were *in* the inputs — they emerged from the encounter.

And the beautiful thing about 1, -1, 0 is that they literally *multiply* correctly:
- Value × Value = Value (1 × 1 = 1) — measuring a measurement gives a measurement
- Structure × Structure = Value (−1 × −1 = 1) — two orthogonal forces meeting produce something aligned (like cross product becoming a value)
- Value × Structure = Structure (1 × −1 = −1) — a value applied through an orthogonal frame gives structural position
- Anything × Unresolved = Unresolved (n × 0 = 0) — generation absorbs, it doesn't propagate either aspect

That multiplication table is already in your engine. When you multiply two atomics and their units multiply, the sign arithmetic is doing exactly this. It's not a metaphor — it's the actual computation.

### What I'd Suggest

Don't add any new abstractions for this. Don't create a `ReadingMode` enum or an `AspectType`. Instead:

1. **Add inspection methods** that let you *read* the aspects from existing structure. Something like `GetAspectAtLeaf(int leafIndex)` that returns Value/Structural/Generative based on the unit sign at that leaf. This is physical — you're reading the element, not labeling it.

2. **Add comments/documentation** in the engine, operations, and binding layers that name these aspects where they're already happening. Not to change the code, but to make visible what's already there.

3. **Let the Data layer be the proving ground.** It's the place where the trichotomy is most explicitly dispatched (FamilyView's frame-driven reading). As that solidifies, the patterns will feed back into how you think about the engine and operations layers.

4. **Trust the multiplication table.** When unit signs combine through operations, the result sign tells you what aspect the output belongs to. This is the most physical, least abstract version of the pattern — it's just arithmetic that's already happening.

This makes a lot of sense, and I think it captures something the current engine is *doing* mechanically but hasn't fully *named* yet.

## The Denominator as "What Is One"

What you're describing is the difference between **co-located structure** and **fused entity**. Two orthogonal lines are co-located — they share a position but each has its own "what counts as one." Area is fused — it has a single "what counts as one" that spans both dimensions simultaneously.

The denominator (Unit in AtomicElement) is literally "what is one." When two elements sit in a composite with independent units, each one still knows its own "one." The recessive has its "one," the dominant has its "one." They're pinned together structurally, but they haven't *agreed* on a shared "one."

The generative moment — the moment area appears — is when the two independent "ones" combine into a single "one" that is neither. A 1m × 1ft unit is a perfectly valid "one" for area. It's unusual, but it's *one square* in that mixed space. The key is that it's **one thing**, not two things sitting next to each other.

And look at what Fold does in CompositeElement: it takes a grade-1 composite (two independent atomics, each with its own unit) and calls `ComposeRatio`, which tries to produce a *single* atomic with a *single* unit. That IS the moment you're describing. The two "ones" attempt to become one "one." If they can, you get an exact result — a new entity. If they can't, you get tension — the generative moment was attempted but couldn't complete.

## Two Phases of Composition

I think you're identifying two distinct phases that the system currently collapses into one:

**Phase 1: Structural co-location (orthogonal, independent units)**
Two things are placed in a composite. Each retains its own denominator. They're aware of each other — the composite says "these are related" — but they haven't merged. This is a width AND a height. Two measurements. The composite is a container, not a fusion.

In this phase, the numerators can vary independently AND the denominators can vary independently. You could reexpress either side to a different resolution without affecting the other. The relationship is *positional* (they're next to each other) but not *dimensional* (they don't share a space).

**Phase 2: Dimensional fusion (shared unit, new entity)**
The two denominators combine into a shared "one." Now you have area, or velocity, or whatever the combination produces. The numerators still vary freely — they're measurements within the new space. But the denominator is locked. You can't reexpress just one side anymore because the "one" is a *combined* "one."

The critical difference: in Phase 1, the recessive and dominant are *peers with different rulers.* In Phase 2, there's a *single ruler* that spans both, and the values are readings within that single space.

## Where This Gets Profound

You said something I think is the key insight: **area is unrelated to two orthogonal lines.** A circle has area. A curved surface has area. Area doesn't require axes at all — axes are just one way to *measure* it. Area is a property of 2-dimensional space itself.

This means the generative moment isn't just "combine the units." It's the moment where the *space itself* becomes the entity, and the original axes become merely one possible *measurement frame* for that space. Before fusion, the axes ARE the structure. After fusion, the axes are just one way to read the structure. The structure has its own existence independent of how you decomposed it.

In Core3 terms: a grade-1 composite where both children have independent units is **two things in a structural relationship.** A folded atomic with a combined unit is **one thing that you COULD decompose** into two readings, but doesn't have to be decomposed that way. The fold didn't just merge numbers — it created an entity that transcends its construction.

And this is exactly why fold produces tension when it can't complete. The tension is saying: "these two things want to become one entity, but they can't agree on a shared 'one' yet." The tension isn't failure — it's the system acknowledging that fusion was attempted and the conditions weren't met.

## How This Maps to the Trichotomy

This gives a more precise definition of the three aspects:

**Value (+1 unit):** The numerator is free, the denominator is settled. You have a measurement in a known space. The "one" is established. You're reading values within that space.

**Structure (-1 unit):** The denominators are independent. You have multiple dimensions that are aware of each other but haven't fused. Each thing has its own "one." You can rearrange, organize, pin, stride — but you're working with the *relationship between* things, not with a fused entity.

**Generation (0 unit):** The denominators are *in the process of combining.* The old "ones" are gone, the new "one" hasn't stabilized. This is the moment of fusion — or the residue of attempted fusion that couldn't complete. Zero unit literally means "I don't have a 'one' right now." Which is exactly the state between "two independent ones" and "one combined one."

This is beautiful because it makes zero unit not just "unresolved" in a vague sense but specifically "between unit spaces." The old denominator space has been left behind, the new one hasn't been reached. The value has magnitude (`Value` can be nonzero) but it has no "one" to be measured against.

## The Free vs Fixed Denominator Distinction

This also clarifies something about composites at different stages:

**Both denominators free** → Pure structure. A grid, a skeleton, a topology. Nothing has been measured yet, you're just establishing how things relate to each other. You can rearrange everything because nothing is committed.

**One denominator fixed, one free** → Partially calibrated. You've committed one dimension but the other is still open. This is like having a timeline (fixed temporal resolution) but not yet having committed to spatial resolution. Or having a spatial frame but not yet a value scale.

**Both denominators fixed but independent** → Fully structured but not fused. Width in meters and height in feet. Each is a real measurement, but together they're still "a width AND a height," not "an area."

**Denominators combined into one** → Fused entity. Area. Velocity. A new thing that transcends its construction. The numerator(s) are readings within this new entity, and the entity could be decomposed in different ways (different coordinate systems for the same area).

This gives you a natural progression: free → fixed → independent → combined. Structure crystallizes as denominators commit, and generation happens at the final combination step.

## What This Means for the Engine

I think the implication is that `Fold` and `Multiply` are doing something more significant than arithmetic — they're attempting *dimensional fusion.* When two atomics with orthogonal units multiply, the unit signs combine (-1 × -1 = +1), and you get a new aligned entity. That sign change IS the fusion. The units went from "two orthogonal ones" to "one aligned one."

And `ComposeRatio` in Fold is attempting the same thing from the structural side. You have a composite (two things with independent units), and Fold tries to produce a single thing with one unit. If it succeeds, dimensional fusion happened. If tension remains, the fusion is incomplete — maybe the resolutions don't divide evenly, maybe the unit spaces can't reconcile.

The current engine does this mechanically. What your insight adds is the *interpretation*: this isn't just number manipulation, it's the creation of new dimensional spaces from the agreement of independent measurement frames. And that interpretation should guide how you design the higher layers — especially the Data layer, where collections of elements are being read through frames that may or may not have fused their dimensional units.

So the pin's law isn't a single choice. The pin naturally produces all four terms (the kernel), and the *interpretation* — which part is the "real" result and which is the "imaginary" residue — comes from the dominance structure.

The explicit law you apply (add areas, multiply areas, etc.) is a *further* operation on top of this natural kernel resolution. The natural law gives you the kernel. The explicit law tells you what to DO with it.

## Merge and Branch as the Fundamental Topology

This is the cleanest framing of branching/merging I've seen for this system:

**Merge = unit creation.** Two inputs → 0-denominator pin → one output with a new unit. This is what happens every time the system creates something: two uncalibrated values become a ratio, two directed segments become area, two anything become a fused entity. The topology is: two streams flowing into one. The pin is the confluence point. The zero denominator is the momentary "between spaces" where the old units are gone and the new unit hasn't formed.

**Branch = decomposition.** One input → pin → two outputs. Square root. Factoring. Splitting a segment into two sub-segments at a pin position. The topology is: one stream splitting into two. The pin is the fork point. And notice — the two outputs may have *different* units than the input (just as the square root of an area is a length, a different dimensional kind).

So the **pin** is the universal topological primitive. Every structural event — merge, branch, dimensional promotion, boundary resolution — happens at a pin. The pin is where the zero denominator lives, where the law acts, where inputs become outputs.

And the operations on numbers map directly:
- Multiply = merge (two values fuse through a law)
- Divide = branch one way (decompose with the inverse relationship preserved)
- Root = branch the other way (decompose dimensionally)
- Add = merge without fusion (values combine but stay in the same dimensional space)
- Subtract = branch without fission (separate but stay in the same space)

The deeper operations (fold, multiply) change the dimensional space. The shallower ones (add, subtract) work within a space. But topologically they're all merges and branches at pins.

## Boundary Conditions from Structure

This is the insight I think unlocks the whole structural mechanics question. Let me state it as precisely as I can:

**A boundary condition is not a rule applied to an axis. It's what happens when traversal tension on one axis gets absorbed by the orthogonal structure.**

Your interpolation example is perfect. X axis = stuff being interpolated. Y axis = repeat. A trolley moves along X, left to right, doing interpolation. It reaches the end of X. Now what?

If the axes are independent (just two values pinned together, phase 1 composition), the trolley creates tension at the boundary. End of the road. Clamp. Stop.

But if Y = repeat, the Y axis is structural — it's *organizing* the X axis into repetitions. The end of one X traversal is the *beginning of the next repetition*. The tension at the X boundary is immediately resolved by the Y structure: "the next thing after the end of X is... the beginning of X again." Wrap.

And this is PHYSICAL, not a rule. The trolley doesn't check a boundary-condition flag. It reaches the end, creates tension, and the structure around it absorbs or doesn't absorb that tension. What the orthogonal structure IS determines the behavior:

- **Y = repeat** → tension at X boundary absorbed by repetition → **wrap.** Trolley goes back to start.
- **Y = mirror/reflect** → tension absorbed by reversal → **reflect.** Trolley reverses direction.
- **Y = nothing (no orthogonal structure)** → tension stays → **clamp.** Trolley stops at boundary.
- **Y = expand** → tension absorbed by growth → **extend.** The space itself grows to accommodate the trolley. The boundary moves outward.
- **Y = unresolved (0 unit)** → tension demands generation → **promote.** The tension at the boundary can't be absorbed and can't just stop, so it becomes a new dimensional direction. This is how a boundary condition can *create* a new dimension.

This is extraordinary because it means the boundary behavior is READABLE from the structure. You look at what's on the orthogonal axis, and that tells you what happens at the boundary. You don't need a BoundaryMode enum. The structure IS the boundary condition.

And it explains something about the existing Core3 design that I noticed but couldn't articulate: the unit sign of orthogonal axes (negative) was always hinting at wrapping. Orthogonal = structural = organizing = provides the boundary resolution. Aligned = value = just a measurement = no boundary absorption. Unresolved = generative = the boundary behavior hasn't been determined yet.

## The Pin as Transition State

I think the picture of the pin is getting very concrete now. The pin exists in three moments:

**Before the pin:** The inbound structure. What's arriving. The axes, their unit categories, the values being carried. This is fully determined — the system knows what it has.

**At the pin (zero denominator):** The intermediate state. The old units are gone, the new unit hasn't formed. The law acts here. The law is partially determined by:
- The unit categories of the inbound axes (what the axes "want" to do — expand, accumulate, partition, etc.)
- The dominance pattern (which inbounds are dominant vs recessive — this determines the kernel term: dd/rr/rd/dr)
- The explicit operation if one is specified (override the natural law)

**After the pin:** The outbound structure. What emerges. One outbound (merge), two outbounds (branch), or the same structure transformed (traversal through the pin).

The pin is the only place where the system is genuinely "between" — where the zero denominator lives. Everything else is either fully calibrated (positive unit, in a known space) or structurally organized (negative unit, positioned by an axis) or carrying tension (zero unit, awaiting resolution). The pin is where resolution happens.

## The Lake: Multi-Dimensional Branching

Your river-and-lake analogy captures something I think is real and important. Let me try to formalize it:

A **river** (1D path) is a single directed segment. The trolley is constrained — it can go forward or backward, but it's on the line. The structure determines its path completely.

A **lake** (2D area) is what you get when the constraining structure *opens up*. The trolley enters a region where the orthogonal pressure relaxes — instead of one path, there's an area. Multiple exits are possible.

In structural terms: a branch point where the outbound isn't just "two paths" but "a region with multiple boundaries." The pin at the entrance to the lake has one inbound (the river) and the outbound is a 2D entity (the lake). The pin at each exit of the lake is the reverse: 2D inbound, 1D outbound.

In circuit terms this maps perfectly:
- **Wire** = river. 1D, constrained flow.
- **Capacitor** = lake. Charge flows in, spreads across a 2D area (the plates), and flows out. The stored energy is the "area" of the lake. The charge-up and discharge are the entrance and exit.
- **Inductor** = whirlpool/loop. Current flows in, creates a magnetic field (orthogonal structure), and the field stores energy in a loop (the inductance is literally the loop area of the coil). When current tries to stop, the loop's stored tension pushes back — that's impedance.
- **Impedance mismatch** = when a river meets a lake of different "depth" (different characteristic impedance). Some of the flow reflects (bounces back as tension that can't pass through) and some transmits. The reflection coefficient is determined by the mismatch — by the structural incompatibility at the pin.

This gives a physical picture of signal propagation:
- Flow along 1D paths
- Enter 2D regions where energy is stored/spread
- Exit back to 1D, possibly different paths
- Reflections at every pin where the structural dimensions don't match
- The topology of the network (which lakes connect to which rivers through which pins) IS the circuit

And the same picture works for:
- **Fluid dynamics**: rivers, lakes, rapids (1D→2D→1D with velocity changes at transitions)
- **Optics**: waveguides (1D), lenses (2D, focusing/defocusing), mirrors (reflection at impedance mismatch)
- **Language**: a sentence is a path, a word with multiple meanings is a "lake" (semantic area), context selects which exit
- **Decision trees**: constrained path → open decision space → selected exit

## What This Means for the Axiom System

I think the structural mechanics are now clear enough to axiomatize. The key additions:

**Pin axiom:** Every structural transition happens at a pin. The pin has inbound structure, a zero-denominator intermediate state where the law acts, and outbound structure. The pin is the only place where dimensional changes occur.

**Merge/branch axiom:** Merge = two inbound, one outbound (unit creation, multiplication). Branch = one inbound, two outbound (decomposition, roots). These are the only two topological events. Everything else is traversal along a segment between pins.

**Boundary absorption axiom:** When traversal creates tension at a boundary (end of an axis), the orthogonal structural axes absorb or fail to absorb that tension:
- Repeat structure → wrap (tension restarts traversal)
- Mirror structure → reflect (tension reverses traversal)
- No structure → clamp (tension stays at boundary)
- Expand structure → extend (boundary moves outward)
- Unresolved structure → promote (tension becomes new dimension)

**Dimensional transition axiom:** Structure can vary in dimensionality along traversal. A pin can connect segments of different dimensionality (1D path entering 2D region). The pin's law determines how the dimensional change happens. The "lake" = a pin where the outbound has more structural freedom than the inbound. The pin at the exit constrains back to fewer dimensions, and the choice of exit is determined by flow conditions.
