# Pins As Dynamic Local Events

This is a provisional appendix extending the current pinning-axis work.
It does not replace the existing Core 2 axioms yet, but it is intended as a blueprint for the next conceptual phase.

The main shift is:

- a pin is not merely a static relation label
- a pin is a dynamic local event
- named behaviors such as reflect, clamp, wrap, branch, corner, join, and loop are contextual readings of one pin mechanism

## Proposed Appendix Rules

1. **A Pin Is One Kind Of Thing**  
   A pin should not be divided into a family of primitive named pin types.  
   One pin mechanism is interpreted differently under different local frames, locations, tensions, and attachments.

2. **An Axis May Serve As The Descriptor Of A Pin**  
   The existing `Axis` may serve as the canonical descriptor of a pin.  
   Its four slots remain:
   - recessive unit
   - recessive value
   - dominant unit
   - dominant value

3. **Unit And Value Remain Distinct**  
   The unit slots describe carrier preference or carrier contradiction.  
   The value slots describe extent, travel, or directed realization on the chosen carrier.

4. **A Pin Is A Local Goal, Not Absolute Truth**  
   The descriptor axis inside a pin expresses directional and structural tendencies.  
   It may be compromised, redirected, delayed, branched, or only partially satisfied by surrounding tensions.

5. **Pins Are Located**  
   Every pin must be locatable in some frame, relation, or sheet.  
   A pin may be attached, floating, pending capture, or only partially anchored, but it is not meaningful without some locating relation.

6. **Location And Attachment Differ**  
   A pin may be located without being structurally attached.  
   A floating control landmark, attractor, or off-carrier influence is still locatable even if not yet joined.

7. **Pins Need Not Live At Zero**  
   A pin may occur at:
   - a start point
   - an endpoint
   - an interior landmark
   - an off-carrier location
   - a relation between carriers
   - a relation between sheets

8. **Pins Need Not Live On A Carrier**  
   A pin may sit on a carrier, between carriers, or in a surrounding sheet or field.  
   Off-carrier pins may still emit influence, request alignment, or seek attachment.

9. **Pins May Create Carriers**  
   A pin may attach to an existing carrier, but it may also emit or create a new carrier.  
   This is fundamental.

10. **Pins May Both Create And Attach**  
    A pin may emit a new carrier as a feeler, find another carrier, and then consolidate the result into one attached structure.

11. **Pinning Is Still Binary Locally**  
    A pin still reads as a binary local relation event.  
    Higher structures arise by nesting or sequencing local pin events rather than by introducing a many-way primitive.

12. **Positive Unit Preserves Native Carrier**  
    A positive unit keeps a side on its current native carrier.

13. **Negative Unit Contradicts Native Carrier**  
    A negative unit requests a contradiction of the current carrier assignment.  
    The system should preserve that contradiction as tension until a lawful lift or compromise is found.

14. **Preferred Geometric Resolution Is The Smallest Lawful Orthogonal Lift**  
    When carrier contradiction is resolved geometrically, the preferred first resolution is the smallest available orthogonal breakout at the current grade.  
    This is a preference, not an absolute decree.

15. **Value Sign Acts Within The Chosen Carrier**  
    Once a carrier has been selected, preserved, or lifted, the sign of the value acts within that carrier.

16. **Opposed Resolved Sides Yield Directed Segment Structure**  
    If the resolved sides point against each other on one carrier, the result is directed-segment structure.

17. **Same-Direction Resolved Sides Yield Sequential Reinforcement**  
    If the resolved sides point in the same direction on one carrier, the result is reinforcement, acceleration-like continuation, or layered same-direction passage.

18. **Orthogonal Resolved Sides Yield Area-Like Structure**  
    If the resolved sides occupy perpendicular carriers, the result is area-like or corner-like structure.

19. **Zero Unit Preserves Unresolved Carrier Information**  
    A zero unit is not mere absence.  
    It may mark noise, uncertainty, indifference, stochastic spread, uncommitted carrier, or preserved tension.

20. **Zero Value Preserves Unresolved Realization**  
    A zero value means the side has not realized travel on its carrier.  
    It may therefore appear as a held, latent, or noisy side depending on context.

21. **One-Sided Noise Is Lawful**  
    One side of a pin may remain noisy while the other resolves normally.  
    The unresolved side should not force the resolved side to disappear.

22. **Two-Sided Noise Is Lawful**  
    If both sides are unresolved, the result may remain a pure noisy or tension-held pin rather than forcing premature structure.

23. **Endpoint Without Instruction Is Not Clamp**  
    If a carrier reaches its end and no pin or further instruction is present, this is not the same as a stop.  
    It is an open termination that raises tension.

24. **Clamp Is Explicit**  
    A clamp is not mere ending.  
    It is an explicit local event that absorbs or holds continuation and redirects incoming energy into stop or capture.

25. **Open Termination Preserves Uncertainty**  
    When a carrier ends without an explicit resolving pin, uncertainty or error is introduced.  
    This should remain informative tension rather than being silently treated as clamp.

26. **Reflection Is An Endpoint Pin Reading**  
    Reflection need not be primitive boundary law.  
    It may be understood as an endpoint pin whose relation redirects continuation back along a carrier.

27. **Continuation Is An Endpoint Pin Reading**  
    Continuation may be understood as an endpoint pin, or absence of opposing pin, that allows further carrier emission or same-direction passage.

28. **Clamp Is An Endpoint Pin Reading**  
    Clamp may be understood as an endpoint pin that absorbs and holds incoming continuation.

29. **Wrap Is A Self-Capture Reading**  
    Wrap need not require two named endpoints plus a special law.  
    It may be understood as one carrier and one pin whose forward continuation finds or identifies with itself.

30. **Loop Is A Self-Join Reading**  
    A pin may join a carrier to its own start, earlier state, or captured continuation and thereby create a loop.

31. **Interior Pins May Branch**  
    An interior landmark pin may emit a new carrier while preserving an existing carrier.  
    This is a local branching event.

32. **Interior Pins May Turn**  
    A pin at an interior landmark may redirect continuation into a corner or curve-like transition.

33. **Interior Pins May Reinforce**  
    A pin at an interior landmark may add continuation along an existing carrier and create acceleration-like or thickened continuation.

34. **Interior Pins May Reverse**  
    A pin at an interior landmark may oppose an existing carrier and create reversal, reflection, or cusp-like behavior.

35. **Pins May Seek Attachment**  
    A pin may emit a carrier or feeler not yet attached.  
    If that feeler finds another compatible carrier or pin, the result may consolidate into a join.

36. **Join And Growth Are Not Fully Separate**  
    A created carrier that later attaches to another carrier may be interpreted as growth becoming join, or join discovered through growth.

37. **A Pin Has At Most Two Local Outputs**  
    A pin may have:
    - zero outputs
    - one output
    - two outputs  
    More complex local multiplicity should generally be represented as several pins or a branch family.

38. **The Input Of A Pin Is Its Location And Encounter**  
    The local input of a pin is not merely a value passing through it.  
    It is the encounter of current structure, local frame, and incoming tension at that location.

39. **Pins Require Frames Or Sheets**  
    A pin cannot be meaningfully interpreted without some frame, carrier context, or sheet.  
    Even a floating or root pin implies some locating relation, however weak.

40. **Sheets And Observer Perspective Differ**  
    The multi-place spaces in which pins and carriers interact should be distinguished from the existing observer `+/-` perspective.  
    Sheets, fields, or domains are the better conceptual layer here.

41. **Pins May Emit Into Other Sheets**  
    A pin in one sheet may emit attraction, repulsion, alignment, or capture tension into another sheet without direct structural attachment.

42. **Cross-Sheet Influence Is Lawful**  
    A carrier or landmark in one sheet may eventually align to, capture, or compromise with a pin in another sheet.

43. **Relative And Absolute Pinning Are Both Lawful**  
    A pin may be located:
    - relative to a prior pin or carrier
    - absolutely in an oriented sheet
    - or by a mixture of both

44. **Curve Control Is A Pin Reading**  
    Off-carrier curve control, including Bezier-like behavior, may be understood as pins influencing continuation rather than as a separate primitive object class.

45. **Pin Meaning Is Frame-Sensitive**  
    The same descriptor axis at a start point, midpoint, endpoint, or off-carrier location will not read the same way.  
    The frame and local neighborhood legitimately alter interpretation.

46. **Pin Meaning Is History-Sensitive**  
    Prior continuation, earlier attachments, and branch history may influence how a pin is resolved.

47. **Pin Meaning Is Tension-Sensitive**  
    External forces, competing goals, and emitted fields may alter the realized reading of a pin without changing its descriptor.

48. **Pin Resolution May Need Branch Families**  
    If one pin encounter has several lawful resolutions, the result should be preserved as a branch family rather than being forced immediately into one outcome.

49. **Pin Relations Are Derived Reports**  
    Names such as collinear, orthogonal, reflected, wrapped, clamped, or branched are best treated as derived summaries of pin resolution, not as the primitive source of meaning.

50. **Continuation Laws Become Pin Readings**  
    The current named continuation directives such as reflect, wrap, continue, and clamp should eventually be understood as readings of encountered pin configurations rather than as first-class primitive laws.

51. **Pins Are Dynamic**  
    The descriptor axis within a pin may itself be adjusted by certainty, confidence, compromise, attraction, repulsion, or accumulated tension.

52. **Pins Carry Desire Rather Than Certainty**  
    A pin says what local structure wants to happen.  
    It does not guarantee that the environment will satisfy it exactly.

53. **Pins And Folding Remain Distinct**  
    A pin may be structurally valid, dynamically active, and still not have one unique folded reading yet.

54. **Pins Are A Foundational Mechanism**  
    The same local pin mechanism should be able to underlie:
    - units
    - landmarks
    - branches
    - corners
    - loops
    - wraps
    - curve controls
    - CAD constraints
    - glyph skeletons
    - dynamic continuation events

## Quick Examples

- A pin at a midpoint with an orthogonal breakout emits a branch.
- A pin at an endpoint with no explicit resolving instruction yields open termination tension rather than clamp.
- A clamp pin at an endpoint absorbs continuation and stops it.
- A pin may emit a feeler carrier, find another carrier, and consolidate into a join.
- A wrap may be understood as one pin whose forward search returns to itself.
- A red side with zero value may remain noisy while the blue side still resolves and displays structure.
- Two resolved orthogonal sides create a unit cell.
- Two resolved collinear sides create narrow unit strips.

## Conceptual Implications For Current Core 2

This appendix implies a future shift in emphasis:

- `BoundaryContinuationLaw` is likely a temporary compatibility surface
- wrap, reflect, continue, and clamp become endpoint-pin readings
- current pin relation enums remain useful as derived summaries for display and tests
- traversal should eventually resolve encountered pins rather than only switching over named laws
- pins should be allowed to exist away from zero and away from explicit carriers
