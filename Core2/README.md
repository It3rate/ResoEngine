# Core 2

Core 2 is a structural math library.

It is meant to support ordinary mathematical work, but it is also aimed at something broader: concepts, language, interpretation, and systems that do not become fully solved in one step. In that sense, Core 2 is not just a numeric library. It is an attempt to build a mathematical substrate for meaning-bearing structure.

What makes it different from a more traditional library is that it tries to preserve distinctions that are usually collapsed away early. A value may carry resolution, perspective, directional duality, pinning intent, branch structure, or unresolved tension. A result may be partially reduced, negotiated, or refined later rather than forced into one immediate flattened answer. This makes the library useful not only for geometry-like problems, but also for language, design, dynamic systems, and experiments where interpretation develops over time.

## Core Ideas

In very simple terms, Core 2 is built around a few recurring ideas:

- values are structured readings, not just bare numbers
- direction comes in paired or dual form rather than a single flat sign convention
- support or resolution matters, not only folded value
- pinning and folding are different stages
- branching and ambiguity are first-class
- perspective changes reading without necessarily changing the underlying thing
- some systems should resolve gradually over time instead of all at once

This is why Core 2 can lean toward language as much as toward traditional math. Language constantly depends on:

- focus
- perspective
- partial information
- competing interpretations
- structure that is not yet fully collapsed

Core 2 tries to give those things a lawful mathematical home.

## Mental Model

One good way to read the library is:

- `Axioms` explains the intended conceptual system
- `Elements` defines the native objects
- `Algebra` defines how those objects act on one another
- `Repetition` handles continuation, repetition, and inverse continuation
- `Branching` handles multiplicity and selection
- `Resolution` handles support and committed result support
- `Units` handles signatures, quantities, and physical interpretation
- higher layers such as `Core2.Symbolics` give the system a symbolic authoring and inspection surface

## Why Resolution And Perspective Matter

A traditional system often treats a denominator as a convenience for arithmetic. Core 2 increasingly treats it as support or resolution: part of the substrate of distinctions being preserved. Likewise, a perspective flip is not merely cosmetic. It changes the reading convention while leaving the underlying thing intact. These choices make the system closer to how measurement, interpretation, and language actually behave.

## Why Pinning Matters

Core 2 does not want local relation to disappear too early. Pinning says that structures are attached or encountered in a certain way. Folding is the later act of turning that relation into one enacted result. Keeping those distinct is important for geometry, traversal, distributed constraints, and eventually for concept and language modeling.

## Why Dynamic Partial Solving Matters

Many interesting systems do not arrive as one closed-form answer. They propose, branch, negotiate, rejoin, and commit over time. Core 2 is meant to support that style of becoming, not just finished results. That is why unresolved tension and branch families are treated as meaningful structure rather than as failures.

## Applicability

The library is general-purpose enough to be used for things like:

- geometry and structural composition
- forces, flow, and traversal
- repetition and inverse continuation
- dynamic execution and partial resolution
- design interpretation
- language and concept modeling

The language goal is especially important. Core 2 is intended to be able to encode structures that ordinary math libraries usually leave outside the system, such as variable focus, competing readings, carried meaning, and partial resolution.

## Suggested Reading Order

If you are new to the codebase, a good first path is:

1. `Axioms`
2. `Elements`
3. `Resolution`
4. `Repetition`
5. `Branching`
6. `Units`

After that, the symbolic and interpretation layers usually make much more sense.
